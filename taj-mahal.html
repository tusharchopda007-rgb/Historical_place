About Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal is an ivory-white marble mausoleum located on the right bank of the river Yamuna in the city of Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India. It was commissioned in 1632 by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan to house the tomb of his favourite wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth. The Taj Mahal is widely recognized as "the jewel of Muslim art in India" and remains one of the world's most celebrated structures and a symbol of India's rich history.

Located in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, approximately 200 kilometers south of New Delhi, the Taj Mahal stands as a testament to Mughal architectural excellence. The construction began in 1632 and took approximately 22 years to complete, involving over 20,000 artisans and craftsmen from across the Mughal Empire and Central Asia. The main mausoleum was completed in 1643, while the surrounding buildings and garden were finished five years later in 1648.

The Taj Mahal is historically important not only as a monument of love but also as a masterpiece of Mughal architecture, combining elements from Islamic, Persian, and Indian architectural styles. It was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 for being "the jewel of Muslim art in India and one of the universally admired masterpieces of the world's heritage." The monument attracts millions of visitors annually and serves as a symbol of India's cultural heritage and architectural brilliance.

"Not a piece of architecture, as other buildings are, but the proud passions of an emperor's love wrought in living stones." - Sir Edwin Arnold

Interesting Facts

  • The Taj Mahal changes color throughout the day - appearing pinkish in the morning, milky white in the evening, and golden at night when illuminated by the moon.
  • The four minarets surrounding the main tomb are tilted slightly outward to protect the main mausoleum from damage in case of an earthquake.
  • Over 1,000 elephants were used to transport building materials during the construction of the Taj Mahal.
  • The intricate calligraphy on the walls includes verses from the Quran, and the letters appear to be the same size from top to bottom despite being progressively larger as they go higher.
  • Shah Jahan planned to build a second Taj Mahal in black marble across the river, but was deposed by his son before he could begin construction.
